## Thesis

The textbook picture of a datacenter network is a single fat-tree Clos: commodity switches arranged in a folded topology that looks non-blocking to every server. That picture is still true for CPU datacenters. It is badly misleading for the GPU datacenters where the hyperscalers actually spend their capex in 2026. A modern AI datacenter is no longer one network; it is three, plus a fourth that leaves the building. A scale-up fabric (NVLink or TPU ICI) behaves like memory, a scale-out backend (InfiniBand, RoCE, or AWS SRD) carries collectives, a frontend Ethernet carries storage and control, and a wide-area SDN carries cross-DC training. The interesting industrial question has stopped being "what topology do you use?" and become "how do you build four topologies that stay out of each other's way?"

## The setup

For about a decade, the dominant mental model of "a datacenter network" was Google's Jupiter. The 2015 [Jupiter Rising](https://conferences.sigcomm.org/sigcomm/2015/pdf/papers/p183.pdf) paper traced five generations of multi-stage Clos fabrics built from merchant silicon with a centralized control plane — by 2015, 30,000+ servers at 40 Gb/s each with 1.3 Pb/s of bisection bandwidth. Meta converged on the same pattern with the [F16 / Minipack](https://engineering.fb.com/2019/03/14/data-center-engineering/f16-minipack/) fabric — 16 parallel 100 G planes per pod, 1.6 Tb/s per rack, chosen over a 4×400 G alternative because 400 G optics supply and power did not cooperate. AWS chose a different transport but the same spirit with [Scalable Reliable Datagram](https://www.amazon.science/publications/a-cloud-optimized-transport-protocol-for-elastic-and-scalable-hpc) (SRD), which sprays packets across ~64 Ethernet paths and reassembles order in Nitro silicon.

That world has a single answer to every topology question: a folded Clos, with merchant-silicon leaf/spine switches, ECMP on 5-tuple, and a congestion-control argument as the only remaining research target. The world that replaced it — the world where a single training run pushes 24,576 GPUs and a single rack pulls 120 kW — does not fit the single-Clos mental model, and the rest of this post is a tour of the four layers that replaced it.

## Part 1 — The industrial playbook

### The CPU baseline has evolved into an optical Clos

The general-purpose CPU fabric did not stay still. Google's 2022 [Jupiter Evolving](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3544216.3544265) paper reports that Jupiter is no longer a Clos at all at the spine layer: aggregation blocks now wire to each other through MEMS-based Optical Circuit Switches (OCSes), with SDN traffic engineering over the top. Over a decade that move delivered 5× capacity, 30% lower capex, 41% lower power, and a 3× faster fabric reconfiguration than the pre-OCS Clos. The current generation [scales to 13 Pb/s of bisection bandwidth](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/networking/speed-scale-reliability-25-years-of-data-center-networking), built from 512×400 G aggregation blocks. Meta and AWS did not go optical, but they did double and redouble the plane count and the per-port rate. The "boring" Ethernet datacenter is still boring in shape, but it now runs at 400–800 Gb/s endpoints over merchant silicon that looks much more like an ASIC co-designed with its own transport.

### Scale-up: the fabric that behaves like memory

The first truly new layer is the scale-up fabric, which ties a small set of GPUs together so tightly that the distinction between "memory" and "network" blurs. NVIDIA's [GB200 NVL72](https://www.nvidia.com/en-us/data-center/gb200-nvl72/) puts 72 Blackwell GPUs into a single NVLink domain using nine NVLink-Switch trays per rack. Each B200 carries 18 NVLink ports at 100 GB/s, giving [1.8 TB/s of bidirectional per-GPU bandwidth](https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/nvidia-gb200-nvl72-delivers-trillion-parameter-llm-training-and-real-time-inference/) — roughly 36× the per-GPU bandwidth of a 400 Gb/s NIC. NVLink-Switch extensions push that to a 576-GPU domain at >1 PB/s aggregate. The number to internalize is the asymmetry: any tensor-parallel or context-parallel axis that fits inside the NVLink domain runs at memory-bandwidth speeds; anything that spills out drops by an order of magnitude.

Google's answer is topologically different. The [TPU v4 supercomputer](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.01433) builds a 4096-chip machine as a 3D torus assembled from 64 cubes of 64 chips, with 48 Palomar 136-port MEMS OCSes configuring the inter-cube links. The OCS and optics together cost under 5% of system cost and under 3% of system power, and the twisted-torus shape the OCS lets them pick buys 1.3–1.6× throughput on all-to-all compared with a plain torus. The successor [TPU v5p](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/introducing-cloud-tpu-v5p-and-ai-hypercomputer) scales this to 8,960 chips per pod with 4,800 Gb/s per-chip ICI and documented [OCS-based "ICI resiliency"](https://docs.cloud.google.com/tpu/docs/v5p) that routes around failed optical links. NVIDIA and Google reached the same conclusion — scale-up is where the fastest fabric belongs — through completely different topologies.

### Scale-out backend: rail-optimized fat-trees, on purpose

Outside the NVLink or ICI domain, training jobs fall off a bandwidth cliff. The industrial response is a dedicated, rail-optimized backend fabric. NVIDIA's [DGX SuperPOD H100 reference architecture](https://docs.nvidia.com/dgx-superpod/reference-architecture-scalable-infrastructure-h100/latest/network-fabrics.html) wires each Scalable Unit of 32 nodes so that for every node, each of its 8 GPU NICs reaches the matching NIC on the other 31 nodes in exactly one switch hop. Inter-rail and inter-SU traffic pays an extra hop at the spine. The upcoming [B300 / Quantum-X800 architecture](https://docs.nvidia.com/dgx-superpod/reference-architecture/scalable-infrastructure-b300-xdr/latest/network-fabrics.html) grows the rail-aligned unit to 72 nodes at 800 Gb/s. That shape is not cosmetic: AllReduce schedules generated by NCCL assume rail alignment, and [Meta's SIGCOMM '24 paper](https://engineering.fb.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/sigcomm24-final246.pdf) on their RoCE deployment reports up to ~40% AllReduce improvement on its 24,576-GPU Llama 3 training clusters once Enhanced-ECMP respects QP affinity and each flow is split across 16 QPs.

Who ships what varies, but the pattern is invariant. [Meta's twin 24K-GPU GenAI clusters](https://engineering.fb.com/2024/03/12/data-center-engineering/building-metas-genai-infrastructure/) run Grand Teton GPUs behind one RoCE backend (Arista + Minipack2) and one InfiniBand Quantum-2 backend — the same platform, two different transports. xAI's 100K-GPU [Colossus](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/spectrum-x-ethernet-networking-xai-colossus) uses NVIDIA Spectrum-X with SN5600 switches at 800 Gb/s, which NVIDIA reports hits 95% effective throughput at three fabric tiers where plain Ethernet tops out near 60%. The underlying physics — rail-aligned fat-tree, high radix per port, one NIC per GPU — is now the industrial baseline for backend design.

### The frontend/backend split is explicit, not implicit

The separation between backend and frontend is no longer an incidental deployment choice; it is an architectural principle written into the reference docs. NVIDIA's [GB200 SuperPOD architecture](https://docs.nvidia.com/dgx-superpod/reference-architecture-scalable-infrastructure-gb200/latest/network-fabrics.html) specifies four fabrics explicitly: a Compute Fabric (InfiniBand or Spectrum-X, rail-optimized), a Storage Fabric (separate IB or RoCEv2), in-band management Ethernet, and an isolated out-of-band management plane. On a GB200 compute tray, four ConnectX-7 NICs sit on the backend and two BlueField-3 DPUs sit on the storage + in-band plane; the split is enforced at the NIC. [Meta's RoCE blog](https://engineering.fb.com/2024/08/05/data-center-engineering/roce-network-distributed-ai-training-at-scale/) makes the same argument from first principles — a dedicated backend lets the AI fabric evolve independently of the general DC network, which is necessary because the traffic matrices are fundamentally different: a few bursty elephants dominated by gradient sync on the backend, many small flows plus large storage reads on the frontend.

### Congestion control is the price of running collectives over lossless Ethernet

The cost of running RDMA on Ethernet instead of InfiniBand shows up as congestion-control complexity. Microsoft's [DCQCN](https://conferences.sigcomm.org/sigcomm/2015/pdf/papers/p523.pdf) and the operational war stories in [RDMA over Commodity Ethernet at Scale](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/rdma_sigcomm2016.pdf) are the canonical references for why PFC is dangerous: PFC forms deadlocks when paused links form directed cycles, and a [pause-frame storm can take down a fabric](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/rdmahotnets16.pdf). Alibaba answered with [HPCC](https://liyuliang001.github.io/publications/hpcc.pdf), which uses in-network telemetry to let senders compute precise rate updates and cuts flow completion time by up to 95% over DCQCN. The research tax of running lossless Ethernet is roughly: one SIGCOMM paper per operational incident.

### Cross-datacenter: SDN WANs, now forking for AI

The fourth network lives between datacenters. Google's [B4](https://conferences.sigcomm.org/sigcomm/2013/papers/sigcomm/p3.pdf) showed in 2013 that a centralized SDN WAN with traffic engineering can push inter-DC links to near 100% utilization — a 2–3× improvement over conventional WAN practice — and the [B4-After](https://research.google/pubs/b4-and-after-managing-hierarchy-partitioning-and-asymmetry-for-availability-and-scale-in-googles-software-defined-wan/) paper reports 100× the traffic a few years later at 33 sites, using hierarchical TE and two-phase flow matching to fit 8× more TE rules into the same switch silicon. Meta runs two distinct backbones: the MPLS-based [Express Backbone (EBB)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3603269.3604860) for DC-to-DC traffic with centralized TE over Open/R, and a separate Classic Backbone for DC-to-POP; their recent [10X Backbone](https://engineering.fb.com/2025/10/16/data-center-engineering/10x-backbone-how-meta-is-scaling-backbone-connectivity-for-ai/) post frames the current scaling pressure as explicitly AI-driven. [AWS's global network](https://aws.amazon.com/about-aws/global-infrastructure/global-network/) runs ~20M km of fiber on custom hardware at 400 GbE with physical-layer encryption on all inter-facility traffic.

The AI-on-WAN case was once theoretical. [Pathways](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.12533) and [PaLM](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.02311) showed it work in practice: PaLM 540B was trained across two TPU v4 pods of 3,072 chips each, with within-pod SPMD over ICI and cross-pod gradient exchange over the ordinary DCN, orchestrated by a single-controller runtime. [Gemini's technical report](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/gemini/gemini_v2_5_report.pdf) confirms multi-pod, multi-DC training is now routine for frontier models. The WAN has quietly become a fourth tier of the training fabric, and it changes how you think about all three tiers below it — the cross-pod phase is the slowest one, so the within-pod phases must be scheduled to avoid stalling it.

### Ultra Ethernet: the re-convergence bet

If the current world has too many transports, the Ultra Ethernet Consortium's [Specification 1.0](https://ultraethernet.org/uec-2025-in-review-preparing-for-what-comes-next-a-letter-from-uecs-chair/), released 11 June 2025, is the industry's attempt to put them back into one box. UEC centers on the Ultra Ethernet Transport (UET), a 562-page stack backed by [AMD, Arista, Broadcom, Cisco, HPE, Intel, Meta, and Microsoft](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/ultra-ethernet-consortium-uec-launches-specification-1-0-transforming-ethernet-for-ai-and-hpc-at-scale-302478685.html). The roadmap points directly at the fault lines of the current backend: congestion management, small-message performance, scale-up transport, and in-network collectives. Whether UEC collapses the scale-up/backend distinction, or merely standardizes the backend, is the single biggest open bet in datacenter networking today.

### Optical Circuit Switches: the fabric-shape knob

Underneath all of this, the hyperscalers have normalized optical circuit switching as a first-class fabric primitive. Google's [Mission Apollo](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.10041) describes the 136×136 Palomar 3D-MEMS OCS, deployed in production since 2013, at ~108 W per switch versus ~3 kW for a comparable-radix packet switch; the [Lightwave Fabrics](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3603269.3604836) SIGCOMM '23 follow-up covers the integrated stack that both Jupiter and the TPU interconnect now share. OCSes are not a research novelty anymore; they are the reason Google can rewire a TPU v5p pod around a failed optical link and keep training going.

## Part 2 — Where research is pushing the topology question

If Part 1 is the consensus, Part 2 is the open front. The interesting recent papers share a common move: they stop treating topology as a deployment-time given and start treating it as a runtime variable.

### Synthesis: topology and collectives, co-designed

[Efficient Direct-Connect Topologies for Collective Communications](../papers/nsdi-2025/efficient-direct-connect-topologies-for-collective-communications.md) (NSDI '25) rejects the premise that you pick a topology and then pick a collective schedule to run on it. It searches the Pareto frontier of (topology, schedule) pairs using property-preserving graph expansions and a polynomial-time BFB schedule generator, and shows up to 56× AllReduce improvement over ShiftedRing at near-1000-node scale — in a regime where NCCL's default schedulers fall off the end of the table. That framing fits optical ML clusters, TPU-style tori, and any fabric where port count is scarce, because it says the right unit of optimization is topology-plus-schedule, not either alone.

### Reconfigurable torus fabrics

[Morphlux](../papers/asplos-2026/reconfigurable-torus-fabrics-for-multi-tenant-ml.md) (ASPLOS '26) pushes photonic programmability down to server scale. It inserts an optical interposer under each 4-accelerator server, then lets a software controller redirect sub-rack bandwidth, stitch non-contiguous free servers into a logical torus, and patch around failed accelerators without evacuating the whole job. On a TPU-cluster simulator driven by public TPU v4 slice distributions, Morphlux eliminates up to 50% of stranded `Y`-dimension bandwidth, successfully serves 32-TPU requests that default TPU/SiPAC allocators reject ~75% of the time, and recovers from single-chip failures in ~1.2 s. The claim is that the torus-versus-flexibility tradeoff dissolves once bandwidth assignment becomes programmable.

### The intra-server fabric is part of the inter-server path

[FuseLink](../papers/osdi-2025/enabling-efficient-gpu-communication-over-multiple-nics-with-fuselink.md) (OSDI '25) attacks a different corner of the same problem: when inter-server traffic is skewed, a GPU can borrow a peer GPU's idle NIC by relaying through NVLink. The mechanism is virtual-memory remapping — the application still writes to an NCCL buffer, but that buffer physically lives on a relay GPU — plus priority-aware scheduling so the NIC's real owner always wins. On 8-GPU servers with 8×400 Gbps NICs, point-to-point inter-server bandwidth goes from 49 GB/s (baseline NCCL+PXN) to 212 GB/s. The paper is the clearest statement of a new idea: the NVLink fabric and the RDMA fabric are two halves of one path, and runtime systems should treat them as such.

### Transport, still under relentless pressure

Nearly every top-venue networking venue publishes multiple papers redesigning the RoCE/InfiniBand transport. [SIRD](../papers/nsdi-2025/sird-a-sender-informed-receiver-driven-datacenter-transport-protocol.md) and [Pyrrha](../papers/nsdi-2025/pyrrha-congestion-root-based-flow-control-to-eliminate-head-of-line-blocking-in-datacenter.md) (NSDI '25) attack HOL blocking from the sender and the receiver sides respectively; [Fork](../papers/eurosys-2025/fork-a-dual-congestion-control-loop-for-small-and-large-flows-in-datacenters.md) (EuroSys '25) runs a dual loop — sender-driven for small flows, receiver credits for elephants. [PrioPlus](../papers/eurosys-2025/enabling-virtual-priority-in-data-center-congestion-control.md) emulates many strict priority queues inside one physical queue using delay channels, avoiding switch changes. [White-boxing RDMA](../papers/nsdi-2025/white-boxing-rdma-with-packet-granular-software-control.md) and [ScalaCN](../papers/nsdi-2025/mitigating-scalability-walls-of-rdma-based-container-networks.md) go lower, exposing RNIC-internal controls so software can steer packet-granular behavior and detect the "scale-induced cliff" that RDMA container fabrics hit. [Söze](../papers/osdi-2025/s-ze-one-network-telemetry-is-all-you-need-for-per-flow-weighted-bandwidth-allocation-at.md) (OSDI '25) shows that one per-packet max queueing-delay signal is enough for decentralized weighted max-min allocation — a minimalist answer to HPCC's heavier telemetry. The uniting theme: the transport is not done, because the topology it runs on is not done.

### Collective tuning and training observability

If topology is variable, the collective library must discover it. [AutoCCL](../papers/nsdi-2025/autoccl-automated-collective-communication-tuning-for-accelerating-distributed-and.md) (NSDI '25) tunes NCCL online per collective task by splitting implementation choices from resource knobs; [MSCCL](../papers/asplos-2026/msccl-rethinking-gpu-communication-abstractions-for-ai-inference.md) (ASPLOS '26) builds collectives from hardware-near channels plus a DSL, so inference gets near-custom comms without vendor-specific stacks. Observability has become a first-class topology problem: [Holmes](../papers/nsdi-2025/holmes-localizing-irregularities-in-llm-training-with-mega-scale-gpu-clusters.md), [Mycroft](../papers/sosp-2025/mycroft-tracing-dependencies-in-collective-communication-towards-reliable-llm-training.md), and [ByteRobust](../papers/sosp-2025/robust-llm-training-infrastructure-at-bytedance.md) all start from the same observation, that a 10K-GPU training job's failure signal first shows up as an odd collective-dependency pattern. [SimAI](../papers/nsdi-2025/simai-unifying-architecture-design-and-performance-tuning-for-large-scale-large-language.md) goes the other direction, reusing real frameworks and NCCL to simulate LLM training at packet granularity with 98.1% alignment to real runs — a tool for reasoning about topologies that don't exist yet.

### Production pressure valves

Finally, two papers attack the edges that the ideal rail-optimized fat-tree story ignores. [OptiReduce](../papers/nsdi-2025/optireduce-resilient-and-tail-optimal-allreduce-for-distributed-deep-learning-in-the-cloud.md) (NSDI '25) shows how to do tail-bounded AllReduce in the public cloud where you cannot assume a dedicated backend. And Google's [hotspot-aware placement paper](../papers/nsdi-2025/preventing-network-bottlenecks-accelerating-datacenter-services-with-hotspot-aware.md) (NSDI '25) cuts persistent ToR hotspots by 90% and Colossus p95 network latency by 50–80%, simply by placing tasks and storage on colder racks. Both are reminders that the "one network, one topology" world is still where most workloads live, and that even there, topology-aware scheduling is the untapped lever.

## The counter-evidence

The three-network thesis is not unchallenged. AWS's [SRD](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/hpc/in-the-search-for-performance-theres-more-than-one-way-to-build-a-network/) is the loudest counter-example. AWS argues — and so far ships — a single Ethernet fabric with a custom transport spread across 64 paths, rather than a separate InfiniBand or RoCE backend. If SRD's approach generalizes to collectives at the scale of GB200-class clusters, the "separate backend" assumption collapses into "one fabric, one smarter transport."

Ultra Ethernet is the second pressure. [UET](https://arxiv.org/html/2508.08906v1) is explicitly trying to make the backend and the general-purpose Ethernet the same network again. If that works, the frontend/backend split becomes a traffic-class boundary, not a physical one, and some of the topology separations in this post stop being load-bearing.

There is also a quieter pushback from the scale-up side. NVLink domains have grown from 8 to 72 to a planned 576 GPUs. Each jump eats territory that used to belong to the scale-out backend: if the NVLink domain can contain your model, the backend only sees data-parallel replicas, and its topology requirements relax. Taken to the limit, "scale-up subsumes scale-out" is a plausible future — the counter-evidence comes from the fact that model sizes also keep growing, and the two curves have stayed uncomfortably close.

None of these objections are strong enough to retire the three-network picture for 2026 designs. But each is a live debate, and a systems architect choosing a topology today should assume the boundaries will move.

## What this means

For the platform builder, the practical consequence is that you cannot pick "a network" anymore. You pick four, and you design for how they share resources: which NIC sits on which plane, which switches are allowed to deadlock under which pause mechanism, which flows belong on which transport. The NVIDIA SuperPOD reference architectures are valuable precisely because they codify this at the connector level.

For the researcher, the interesting topology work has moved up the stack. Graph-and-schedule co-design (the direct-connect paper), programmable photonics (Morphlux), and runtime-adaptive path selection (FuseLink) are all asking the same question: if topology is a variable, what is the control loop? The older Clos-versus-fat-tree argument is genuinely over for CPU fleets. For AI fleets, it is being replaced by a more interesting argument about reconfigurability.

For the field, the next two years will decide whether the industry keeps four networks or collapses them back into one. Ultra Ethernet is the loudest re-convergence bet; NVLink's expansion is a quieter one. Whichever wins, the "datacenter network topology" of 2028 will not look like the 2015 Jupiter picture. It will either be four tiers that finally agree on a shared transport, or it will be one tier that finally agrees on everything — and the research agenda is already shaped by which of those we end up with.
